The Pennsylvania Thesis

A statewide Pennsylvania number is indefensible.

Pennsylvania is not one market but three divergent economies that behave oppositely in the same asset class at the same time. Greater Philadelphia runs on eds-and-meds, pharma and cell-and-gene therapy, finance, and the adjacent Lehigh Valley and Central PA warehouse belt. Greater Pittsburgh runs on post-steel eds-and-meds, robotics and AI, and the Marcellus and Utica gas and petrochemical complex. Between and beyond them sits the vast rural "T" — agriculture, Amish country, Marcellus Shale gas, and small metros. The clearest illustration is a single asset class pulling in opposite directions barely seventy-five miles apart: industrial rents in Scranton rose sixteen percent year over year while the adjacent Lehigh Valley cooled.5

Two structural facts compound the problem. First, Pennsylvania is a non-CON state: its Certificate of Need law sunset on December 18, 1996,21 so hospital, ambulatory surgery center, and nursing-home supply is market-driven, which elevates oversupply risk and forces the feasibility study to carry the full demand burden with no regulatory scarcity to lean on. Second, the demographics demand conservative capture-rate discipline. Pennsylvania added just over thirteen thousand people, a 0.1 percent bump, between July 2024 and July 2025 to roughly 13.1 million, essentially all of it from net international migration, and the state recorded roughly ten thousand more deaths than births.12 Assuming Sun-Belt-style absorption in a flat, aging Commonwealth is a core failure mode.

What follows is organized as a working desk: a live oversupply and digestion monitor, a funding-routing map split East versus West, the review failures that sink Pennsylvania studies, the regulatory edges that decide outcomes, and a per-metro demand fingerprint. Every figure is dated and attributed in the sources below.

Industrial: the Lehigh Valley–Central PA belt is digesting a record cycle

The Lehigh Valley and Central Pennsylvania together form one of the largest and most important warehouse and distribution markets in the country, prized for one-day trucking access to a large share of the US population. After explosive growth from 2020 to 2023, the I-78/I-81 corridor moved closer to peak vacancy through 2025 as it absorbed record new supply.5 As of Q1 2026, Central PA vacancy stood at 6.7 percent, up from below six percent a quarter earlier but still under the 7.0-to-7.5 percent healthy-market band, while the Lehigh Valley eased to 7.4 percent from 8.73 percent in Q4 2025 on 2.6 million square feet leased in the quarter. The Greater Philadelphia industrial region overall ran 8.44 percent, with Class A at 10.93 percent, even as it recorded 5.45 million square feet of positive net absorption in Q1 2026 and 17.46 million over the trailing year.4 CBRE tracked nearly twelve million square feet occupied across the first three quarters of 2025 against 14.3 million in the same 2024 window, with the construction pipeline above ten million square feet for the first time.5 This is the single most important underwriting caution in the Monitor: a market recovering unevenly, with meaningful dark space and near-term availability.

Multifamily: Philadelphia is past its supply peak and holding

Greater Philadelphia delivered its heaviest supply in more than a decade in 2025, roughly 8,300 units, yet occupancy finished the year slightly higher year over year and September occupancy was 95.6 percent against a 94.7 percent national rate.7 Average advertised asking rent reached $1,840 through October 2025, up 2.2 percent and the best-performing Mid-Atlantic metro, with Class B vacancy at just 4.0 percent; the stress is concentrated and predictable, as the Frankford-Kensington corridor rose to 10.1 percent vacancy on the region's heaviest new-supply share, and suburban rents outran the urban core by nearly three points to under one.6 Completions are projected to fall to roughly 7,000 units in 2026, about three-quarters of them in the urban core.6 Pittsburgh is comparatively restrained: asking rents of $1,454 through August 2025, up 0.4 percent, on 95.4 percent stabilized occupancy and 18,200 net new jobs, which supports a balanced-to-undersupplied read.7

Self-storage: Philadelphia is undersupplied per capita but softening

National street rates for a ten-by-ten non-climate-controlled unit averaged $118 in March 2026, down 2.5 percent year over year, against a supply benchmark near 7.0 to 7.8 net rentable square feet per capita. Philadelphia is markedly undersupplied at 3.4 square feet per capita across eighty-nine facilities, with the average ten-by-ten renting at $137 a month, down 1.4 percent, and only a modest, declining pipeline of about 195,000 square feet for 2026.8 The low per-capita figure is a demand cushion, but softening street rates keep the read at balanced rather than tight, and this is one asset class where a statewide per-capita assumption should never be substituted for a submarket count.

Office: a Center City and a Pittsburgh-CBD story

Philadelphia Center City office vacancy sits near twenty percent amid persistent flight-to-quality, with the landmark Wanamaker Building having entered receivership.9 The distinctive sub-sector is life-science lab conversion in University City and the Navy Yard, but that space is itself in a high-vacancy digestion phase: University City lab vacancy reached 39.1 percent as the regionwide pipeline fell from a 2.5-million-square-foot peak in late 2022 to about 500,000 square feet.10 Pittsburgh shows a sharp CBD-versus-metro split. Downtown all-class vacancy hit 24.2 percent in Q3 2025, up from 19.5 percent a year earlier and 16.9 percent pre-pandemic, with lower-tier CBD space above thirty-four percent; metro-wide figures run far lower, near 17.2 to 17.4 percent, with two consecutive quarters of strengthening demand.11 The vendor gap between CBD-only and metro-wide scope is a disclosure item, not a contradiction.

Hotels: a once-in-a-generation 2026 Philadelphia calendar

2026 is a massive Philadelphia tourism year: America250 celebrations, six FIFA World Cup matches from June 14 to July 4, MLB All-Star Week from July 10 to 14, and the PGA Championship in Delaware County. The Philadelphia Convention and Visitors Bureau projects more than 1.5 million overnight visitors spending north of $950 million for a projected $1 billion impact, and Tourism Economics projected a 2.8 percent occupancy boost.1213 But event-driven spikes are transient. Full-year 2025 occupancy ran just over 64 percent, roughly eight percent below 2019, even as spring 2026 weekly snapshots reached 75.3 percent the week ending June 6;14 days before World Cup kickoff, many of Center City's 14,500 rooms remained available and FIFA room-block cancellations were reported.15 An event-year RevPAR spike must be underwritten conservatively, not straight-lined.

Senior housing and data centers: the demand poles

Pennsylvania has one of the oldest populations in the country, and residents over sixty-five could soon outnumber those under twenty — a durable senior-housing fundamental in a state with no CON barrier. National occupancy reached 89.5 percent in Q1 2026, a nineteenth consecutive quarterly increase, with units under construction at their lowest since 2012.20 At the other pole, data centers are the defining emerging theme, led by the more-than-$10-billion Homer City conversion in Indiana County into up to 4.5 gigawatts of gas-fired generation serving a data-center campus, with first power expected in early 2028.18 That build is both an industrial-demand opportunity and, through record PJM capacity pricing, a direct cost-escalation risk for every power-sensitive project in the state.

Common Review Failures

How Pennsylvania feasibility studies fail review.

Each failure below is tied to a real Pennsylvania number. These are the recurring reasons a Pennsylvania study loses credibility with a lender or agency, engineered out of our deliverables before they ship.

  1. Statewide-average error

    Chester County grew 4.9 percent from 2020 to 2024 while Philadelphia County lost 29,878 residents and Allegheny County lost 18,769 over the same period. A statewide multifamily or retail absorption assumption blends growth and decline into a meaningless average.2

  2. Oversupply blindness

    Underwriting 2021-era warehouse absorption and rent growth into a Lehigh Valley and Central PA market that has moved closer to peak vacancy, with a ten-million-square-foot-plus pipeline and Lehigh Valley vacancy that touched 8.73 percent before easing to 7.4 percent, is the signature failure mode. Lease-up curves and concession assumptions must reflect it.45

  3. Shrinking- and aging-market capture-rate error

    Pennsylvania added just 0.1 percent to its population in a year, and only 1.2 percent over the past decade, ranking 41st among the states, with roughly ten thousand more deaths than births. Older industrial metros and rural counties are flat-to-shrinking, with Greene County down 5.5 percent, so Sun-Belt absorption assumptions are indefensible even where collar counties like Cumberland and Chester are genuinely growing.123

  4. Philadelphia tax mispricing

    The ten-year residential tax abatement now phases down from 100 percent in year one to zero by year ten for applications on or after January 1, 2022, the commercial abatement was cut to a flat 90 percent, and a one-percent residential construction-impact tax applies. Layered with the Use and Occupancy tax, BIRT, the wage tax, and ongoing reassessment, these materially change residual land value and NOI.27

  5. Energy and power mispricing

    The PJM 2027/28 capacity auction cleared at a record $333.44 per MW-day, up from $28.92 in 2024, with data-center load accounting for 40 percent, or $6.5 billion, of the December auction's $16.4 billion cost. For power-sensitive projects, utility cost must be underwritten to current PJM reality, not historical rates.1617

  6. Construction-cost and prevailing-wage mispricing

    Pennsylvania's prevailing-wage law applies to public work, and both Philadelphia and Pittsburgh are strong union-labor markets; the Shell Monaca petrochemical build peaked above 6,500 construction workers drawing heavily on the organized building trades. A study that prices labor at open-shop assumptions understates the budget.19

  7. Flood and environmental blind spots

    Pennsylvania carries significant riverine-flood exposure along the Susquehanna, Delaware, and Ohio-Monongahela-Allegheny systems, legacy industrial brownfields such as the former zinc smelter beneath the Shell Monaca site, and mine-subsidence risk in the coal regions. We frame these as a third-party risk flag requiring a qualified environmental assessment, never as a feasibility conclusion.19

Regulatory Edges

The Pennsylvania rules that decide feasibility outcomes.

Four regulatory realities separate a Pennsylvania study that survives review from one that does not. The first is the one competitors most often state wrong.

No Certificate of Need — the law sunset in 1996

Pennsylvania is a non-CON state. The Certificate of Need provisions of the Health Care Facilities Act lapsed on December 18, 1996, when the legislative session ended without reauthorization, leaving Pennsylvania among roughly a dozen states with no CON regime.21 The feasibility implication matches Texas and is the opposite of New York: with no CON barrier, the supply of hospitals, ambulatory surgery centers, and nursing homes is market-driven, oversupply risk is therefore elevated, and a healthcare-facility study must carry the full demand burden because there is no regulatory scarcity value to lean on. Two nuances matter. Nursing homes still require licensure from the Pennsylvania Department of Health, which pledged to enforce licensing strictly after the sunset, and Medicaid reimbursement economics — including recently increased minimum staffing ratios and direct-care hours that constrain effective bed supply — remain material.22 Any competitor claiming Pennsylvania requires CON, likely confusing it with New York or citing pre-1997 data, is simply wrong.

Record PJM capacity pricing and the data-center rush

The PJM 2027/28 capacity auction cleared at a record $333.44 per MW-day, following $329.17 for 2026/27 and $269.92 for 2025/26, up from just $28.92 per MW-day in 2024. Data-center load drove 40 percent, or $6.5 billion, of the December auction's $16.4 billion cost, much of it tied to facilities not yet built.1617 For power-sensitive projects this is a direct, escalating operating-cost input; conversely, the PJM interconnection queue and Marcellus gas availability are the enabling constraint for the western Pennsylvania data-center land rush, from Homer City to TecFusions in Westmoreland County.18 Underwrite utility costs to current PJM reality.

The Philadelphia tax stack

For any new construction in Philadelphia, the tax layer is a first-order feasibility variable. The ten-year residential abatement now declines ten points a year to zero by year ten, the commercial and industrial abatement is a flat 90 percent, and a one-percent residential construction-impact tax applies to permits pulled on or after January 1, 2022.27 Combined with the Use and Occupancy tax, the Business Income and Receipts Tax, the wage tax, and ongoing citywide reassessment, these reshape residual land value and stabilized NOI. In the west, Allegheny County reassessment and common-level-ratio dynamics shift effective millage and should be confirmed against a primary source before a figure is asserted.

Tailwinds in the sponsor's favor

Several recent changes cut the other way. Pennsylvania's Corporate Net Income Tax fell to 7.49 percent for 2026, down from 7.99 percent, and continues phasing toward 4.99 percent by 2031 under Act 53 of 2022;28 the SBA doubled its combined 7(a)-plus-504 ceiling to $10 million effective July 4, 2026, materially enlarging bankable deal size; and fiscal 2026 SBA fee waivers for manufacturing NAICS codes sweeten owner-occupied industrial deals through September 30, 2026.26

Logistics, state government, and distribution, with Cumberland County up 6.2 percent. The corridor's industrial bright spot at 6.7 percent vacancy, though still absorbing record new supply.45

Logistics, healthcare, and legacy industry across Northeastern Pennsylvania. A flat-to-soft population, yet the anchor divergence case: industrial rents rose 16 percent year over year even as the adjacent Lehigh Valley cooled.5

Agriculture & manufacturing

Lancaster

Agriculture, manufacturing, and tourism in one of the state's genuinely growing markets, up more than 10,000 residents. A distinct demand base that a Philadelphia or Harrisburg average would misread.2

Manufacturing & insurance

Erie

Manufacturing, healthcare, and insurance on the lake. A shrinking, older market where absorption is thin and capture-rate discipline governs; we build these studies with primary local research.

University

State College

A pocket market dominated by Penn State: multifamily demand tracks enrollment and pre-leasing rather than metro averages, and student housing is analyzed university-by-university, not statewide.

Pennsylvania Questions

Pennsylvania feasibility study questions.

Does Pennsylvania require a feasibility study for an SBA loan?

Under SBA SOP 50 10 8, a feasibility study is discretionary rather than universally mandated, and lenders commonly require one for special-purpose properties and startup or ground-up projects that lack operating history. Pennsylvania deals frequently involve special-purpose collateral such as hotels, self-storage, and senior housing, so feasibility analysis is regularly expected on Pennsylvania SBA credits.

Does Pennsylvania have a Certificate of Need law?

No. Pennsylvania's Certificate of Need program sunset on December 18, 1996, when the CON provisions of the Health Care Facilities Act lapsed. Hospital, ambulatory surgery center, and nursing-home supply is therefore market-driven, and oversupply risk is elevated because there is no regulatory scarcity value. Nursing homes still require Pennsylvania Department of Health licensure, and Medicaid reimbursement and staffing rules remain material. Any competitor claiming Pennsylvania requires CON is wrong.

Which Pennsylvania real estate markets are oversupplied or digesting right now?

As of Q1 2026, the Lehigh Valley and Central Pennsylvania industrial belt is digesting a record supply cycle, with Lehigh Valley vacancy at 7.4 percent and Central PA at 6.7 percent. Philadelphia Center City office is oversupplied near 20 percent, and University City life-science lab space is digesting at 39.1 percent vacancy. Pittsburgh CBD office is oversupplied at 24.2 percent. Philadelphia multifamily is balanced, past its supply peak.

Who funds SBA and USDA loans in Pennsylvania?

Pennsylvania is served by two SBA district offices, Eastern Pennsylvania in Philadelphia and Western Pennsylvania in Pittsburgh, plus region-specific 504 Certified Development Companies. Huntington National Bank is the largest 7(a) originator by volume in the state. USDA Business and Industry loans route through the Rural Development state office in Harrisburg, which serves 3.4 million rural residents. Pennsylvania businesses received about $1.09 billion in 7(a) approvals across 2,416 loans in fiscal 2025.

What is a targeted employment area in Pennsylvania for EB-5?

A targeted employment area qualifies an EB-5 project for the $800,000 minimum investment. In Pennsylvania, rural areas outside any metropolitan statistical area and outside any city of 20,000 or more, which covers most of the rural "T," qualify as rural TEAs with a 20 percent visa set-aside, while high-unemployment census tracts in Philadelphia and Pittsburgh can qualify as high-unemployment TEAs. TEA status is verified at the filing date using current unemployment data.

How is a Pennsylvania feasibility study different from a national one?

Pennsylvania is three divergent economies: Greater Philadelphia, Greater Pittsburgh, and the vast rural "T." The same asset class moves in opposite directions within the state, as when Scranton industrial rents rose 16 percent year over year while the adjacent Lehigh Valley cooled. A defensible Pennsylvania study is built metro-by-metro against the current supply pipeline, the Eastern-versus-Western funding channel, and Pennsylvania-specific factors like the absence of a Certificate of Need law, record PJM capacity pricing, and the Philadelphia tax-abatement phase-down.
