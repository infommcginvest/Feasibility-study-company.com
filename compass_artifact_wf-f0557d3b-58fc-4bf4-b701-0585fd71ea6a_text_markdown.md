# Restaurants & Foodservice Feasibility-Study Monitor: An Institutional Research-Desk Resource for Lenders, SBA/USDA Underwriters, and Developers

*This monitor is an institutional research-desk resource prepared for lenders, SBA and USDA underwriters, conventional/bank lenders, and developers evaluating restaurant and foodservice going-concern credits. It is the eighth in a series of asset-class monitors. Every quantitative figure carries a named source and a date. Unverifiable items are flagged explicitly. Metrics are labeled by basis: full-service restaurant (FSR) vs. quick-service restaurant (QSR) vs. fast-casual; franchised vs. independent; same-store sales vs. total sales; food cost vs. labor cost as a percentage of sales; unit-level vs. corporate; nominal vs. real (inflation-adjusted) sales growth; and going-concern (business enterprise) value vs. single-tenant net-lease (STNL) real-estate value. Figures on different bases are not compared directly. An operating restaurant is treated throughout as a GOING CONCERN (real estate or leasehold + FF&E/equipment + business/goodwill), which is how it is valued and lent against. A STNL QSR net-lease property is a distinct asset: real estate valued on the tenant's credit and lease, not an operating business.*

## TL;DR

- **The U.S. restaurant industry is large, recovered, and structurally thin-margin.** The National Restaurant Association projects foodservice sales of $1.5 trillion in 2025 with employment of 15.9 million, yet net margins run 3–9%, and 42% of operators reported their businesses were not profitable in 2025 (NRA 2026 State of the Restaurant Industry). Restaurants remain the highest-default sector in SBA lending history. Feasibility, not appetite, is the constraint.

- **The franchised-vs-independent divide is the single biggest driver of feasibility risk.** Franchised QSR concepts offer FDD Item 19 financial-performance benchmarks and higher underwriting confidence; independents have no proven unit economics. SBA loan-performance data shows the split is real but nuanced: limited-service restaurants (NAICS 722211) show a 19.8% historical default rate vs. 4.4% for full-service (NAICS 722511) in one large database, while academic evidence finds only a marginal survival gap between franchise and independent operators.

- **Post-COVID recovery is complete on a dollar basis but incomplete on traffic.** Same-store sales growth in 2024–2025 has been driven overwhelmingly by price and menu mix, not guest counts. Same-store traffic has been persistently negative year-over-year through most of 2025, and full-service on-premises traffic remains below pre-pandemic levels.

- **Labor and food cost inflation define the current underwriting environment.** The National Restaurant Association estimates food costs and labor costs have each risen 35% for the average restaurant over the last five years. California's $20 fast-food minimum wage (effective April 1, 2024) reset the QSR prime-cost floor in that state and is a template other jurisdictions may follow. Prime cost (food + labor) discipline under roughly 60–65% of sales is the fulcrum of restaurant viability.

- **Restaurants are a going-concern credit and one of the most common SBA 7(a) uses.** The 7(a) program funds working capital, leasehold improvements, equipment, franchise fees, and goodwill, which is why it dominates restaurant lending over 504. The SBA Franchise Directory was reinstated under SOP 50 10 8 effective June 1, 2025; franchise brands must be listed for streamlined eligibility.

## Key Findings

1. **Industry size and structure.** The National Restaurant Association's 2025 State of the Restaurant Industry report (published February 6, 2025) projects total foodservice sales of $1.5 trillion in 2025 (about 4% nominal growth) and employment of 15.9 million, adding roughly 200,000 jobs. The industry comprises more than 1 million restaurant and foodservice outlets and is the nation's second-largest private-sector employer.

2. **Segment split is now nearly even between full-service and limited-service.** USDA Economic Research Service data (2024) shows full-service restaurants supplied $552.7 billion and limited-service $550.7 billion of foodservice food sales; full-service edged ahead of limited-service for the first time since the pandemic (36.4% vs. 36.3% of food-away-from-home spending).

3. **The failure-rate "90% myth" is false; the real numbers are more nuanced.** The landmark Parsa et al. study (Cornell Hotel and Restaurant Administration Quarterly, 2005) found 26.16% of independent restaurants failed in year one, roughly 19% in year two, and 14% in year three, with cumulative three-year closure of about 57–62%. First-year failure is closer to 17–30%, not 90%.

4. **Cost structure is thin and prime-cost-driven.** Food cost (COGS) typically runs 28–35% of sales and labor 25–36%, with combined "prime cost" targeted under roughly 60–65% (QSR lower, FSR higher). Occupancy should stay around 6–10% of sales. Net margins run 3–9%.

5. **Restaurants are the highest-default SBA category, but franchised limited-service and full-service differ sharply.** PeerSense's SBA loan database (records 1992–2025) shows a 19.8% historical default rate for limited-service restaurants (NAICS 722211) vs. 4.4% for full-service (NAICS 722511); the divergence reflects loan-term and structure differences as much as concept risk.

6. **Traffic is negative; growth is price-led.** Black Box Intelligence data through 2025 shows industry same-store sales positive (roughly +1% to +2.3% in mid-2025) but same-store traffic persistently negative year-over-year, confirming check growth is driving the top line.

7. **STNL QSR net-lease cap rates are a real-estate basis, distinct from going-concern value.** The Boulder Group's Q2 2025 Net Lease Research Report put overall single-tenant net-lease cap rates at 6.79%, with corporate QSR credits such as McDonald's (4.30%–4.60% on 15-year, per Boulder Group Q1 2026) and Chick-fil-A (4.20%–4.50%) trading in the low-to-mid-4% range. This values real estate on tenant credit, not the operating business.

8. **Franchising is a large, growing, financing-enabling structure.** The IFA/FRANdata 2025 Franchising Economic Outlook (February 5, 2025) projects 851,000 U.S. franchise establishments in 2025, roughly 9 million jobs, and $936.4 billion of franchise output. Franchised QSR is among the most-financed SBA categories.

## Details

### PART 1 — The National Supply & Demand Monitor

#### Industry size, structure, and segmentation

The National Restaurant Association's 2025 State of the Restaurant Industry report (February 6, 2025) forecasts total foodservice-industry sales of $1.5 trillion in 2025, up more than 4% nominally, with restaurant-and-foodservice employment reaching 15.9 million (adding about 200,000 jobs). The restaurant industry surpassed $1 trillion in restaurant-specific sales for the first time in 2024. The Association's report is built from operator and consumer surveys across six segments: family dining, casual dining, fine dining, quick service, fast casual, and coffee/snack. The 2025 report adopted a new sales-estimation methodology based on the USDA Food Expenditure Series, capturing establishments with and without payroll, so headline sales are not strictly comparable to pre-2025 NRA figures (a basis change lenders should note).

The count of establishments varies sharply by definition, which underwriters must respect. Using the federal NAICS 722 ("Food Services and Drinking Places") classification of payroll establishments, the count is roughly 720,000–730,000. Using the broader industry definition including all outlets, the figure exceeds 1 million. These are different bases and should not be blended.

Segment economics diverge sharply and must be labeled:

- **Full-service restaurants (FSR):** table service, higher buildout and labor, prime cost typically 60–65%, longer ramp and breakeven (often 3–5 years). USDA ERS single-location FSR count was 150,115 establishments (2024).
- **Quick-service restaurants (QSR):** counter service, drive-thru, lower labor intensity, prime cost target closer to 55–60%, faster breakeven (often 1–2 years).
- **Fast-casual:** between QSR and FSR, $11–$18 ticket, no waitstaff, AUV of $3 million to $4.5 million on strong brands per Terrapin Construction Group (2026).
- **Franchised vs. independent:** the central risk axis, addressed throughout.
- **Ghost kitchens / virtual brands:** delivery-only, capital-light, but structurally challenged (see below).
- **STNL QSR real estate:** a real-estate investment, not an operating business.

USDA ERS (2024) confirms the segment split has essentially converged: full-service supplied $552.7 billion and limited-service $550.7 billion of foodservice food sales in 2024, with full-service (36.4%) edging past limited-service (36.3%) of food-away-from-home spending for the first time since the pandemic. Limited-service share had peaked at 37.8% in 2020 during the pandemic's off-premise surge.

#### Traffic and same-store sales trends: price, not guests

The defining feature of the 2023–2025 recovery is that growth is price-led, not traffic-led. Black Box Intelligence's monthly reviews through 2025 show:

- Same-store sales positive but modest: roughly +1.1% (April 2025), +1.4% (May), +2.0% (June), +2.3% (August 2025).
- Same-store traffic persistently negative: -1.5% (April), -1.0% (May), -0.9% (June), -0.2% (August 2025).
- Segment rotation: Casual Dining led 2025 same-store sales growth (partly on easy year-over-year comparisons); Fine Dining and Family Dining lagged. Black Box's forward guidance called for continued softening into Q4 2025 and a weak first half of 2026.
- Only about 43–47% of tracked brands posted positive same-store sales in mid-2025, indicating a widening gap between winners and laggards.

Black Box's December 2025 review (cited via The Level Index / Level CFO) found top-line growth was almost entirely price- and mix-driven. This matters for underwriting: a forecast that assumes traffic growth on top of price growth is doubly optimistic in the current environment.

The NRA reports off-premise sales now account for a larger share of revenue than 2019 for about half of operators, but 90% of fine-dining and 87% of casual-dining operators say building on-premises business is the priority.

#### The real story on restaurant failure rates

The "90% fail in year one" claim is a myth that the academic literature has repeatedly debunked, and lenders should not underwrite to it. The definitive source is Parsa, Self, Njite & King, "Why Restaurants Fail," Cornell Hotel and Restaurant Administration Quarterly, Vol. 46, No. 3 (2005), pp. 304–322:

- **First-year failure: 26.16% of independent restaurants** (not 90%).
- Second-year: about 19%; third-year: about 14%.
- Cumulative three-year closure: roughly 57–61% (the Columbus, Ohio longitudinal sample, 1996–1999, using Dun & Bradstreet data).
- **Franchise vs. independent: only a marginal difference** (franchise chains 57.2% vs. independents 61.4% over three years).

Ohio State University's summary of the Parsa work (news.osu.edu) quotes Parsa directly: "The 90 percent figure seems to be a myth, a myth that is harmful to the restaurant industry. If lenders believe this myth, it makes it difficult for serious entrepreneurs to get funding." A separate analysis (Luo & Stark, 2014) of roughly 81,000 restaurants found a 17% first-year failure rate for independent full-service restaurants. The Bureau of Labor Statistics reports roughly 17.5% of accommodation-and-food-service establishments close in year one.

The key underwriting takeaway: restaurant failure is real and cumulative (a majority of independents close within 3–5 years), but front-loaded first-year catastrophe is overstated. Methodology matters enormously: "closure" includes voluntary sale, retirement, and ownership turnover, not only financial failure. Studies vary widely; this is genuinely contested data and should be flagged as such.

#### Cost structure in depth

Restaurant viability is governed by "prime cost," the sum of COGS (food and beverage) plus total labor, expressed as a percentage of sales:

- **Food cost (COGS):** typically 28–35% of sales; industry-average food cost figure of about 32.4% (per WhippleWood's April 2026 benchmark citing NRA 2026). QSR runs lower via standardized purchasing; fine dining runs higher but compensates with pricing.
- **Labor cost:** roughly 25–36% of sales. Per the NRA's 2025 Restaurant Operations Data Abstract (released August 2025, based on 900+ operators), full-service salaries and wages including benefits ran a median of 36.5% of sales in 2024; for full-service operators reporting a pre-tax profit in 2024, labor was a median of 34.2% of sales. QSR labor is often closer to 25%.
- **Prime cost target:** 55–65% of sales. Baker Tilly and Restaurant365 guidance: FSR 60–65%, QSR 55–60%; above 65% "makes it extremely difficult to make a profit." The NRA's 2025 Data Abstract reported limited-service prime cost at a median of about 65 cents of every sales dollar.
- **Occupancy cost:** rent and facility costs should ideally be 5–10% of revenue; above roughly 7–8% in urban markets is a red flag.
- **Net margins:** 3–9% overall. The NRA's 2025 Data Abstract reported full-service median income before taxes of 2.8% and limited-service of 4.0% (2024).

A critical profitability datapoint: 42% of operators reported their businesses were not profitable in 2025, and 60% said overall business conditions deteriorated in 2025 (NRA 2026 State of the Restaurant Industry report). Loss-making full-service operators ran labor cost about 8.7 points higher than profitable peers (42.9% vs. 34.2%, per NRA data cited via The Level Index). This confirms that the contestable margin for most operators is prime cost, especially labor leverage.

#### Wage and food-cost inflation

Operating costs have risen sharply since 2019. The National Restaurant Association states that over the last five years, food costs and labor costs for the average restaurant have each gone up 35%, with double-digit percentage increases across all major cost categories since 2019. On food-away-from-home inflation (a menu-price basis, distinct from wholesale food cost):

- Food-away-from-home CPI rose 4.1% in 2024 and 3.8% in 2025 (USDA ERS), both above the 20-year historical average of about 3.5%.
- Food-away-from-home is forecast to rise about 3.6% in 2026 (USDA ERS).
- Restaurant menu-price inflation peaked at 8.8% year-over-year in March 2023 (NRA), the fastest in more than two decades, and had moderated to about 3.8% year-over-year by mid-2025, still roughly 40 basis points above the 23-year historical average of 3.4% (NRA menu-price index via Nation's Restaurant News / Kalinowski Equity Research, June 2025).
- The gap between restaurant inflation and grocery inflation (food-at-home rose about 2.3% in 2025 per BLS/ERS) affects the "eat out vs. cook at home" value calculus that drives traffic.

Wholesale input volatility remains a risk: farm-level cattle prices were up 16.9% year-over-year in May 2026 and wholesale beef up 15.9% (USDA ERS), and egg prices have swung violently with avian influenza.

#### The delivery / off-premise / ghost-kitchen shift

Third-party delivery is a structural margin drag. DoorDash, Uber Eats, and Grubhub commissions run 15–30% of order value (DoorDash tiers: Basic 15%, Plus 25%, Premier 30%); effective all-in cost often reaches 30–40% once processing, promotions, and refunds are included. Against 3–5% net margins, an un-repriced delivery order can erase the profit on the plate. To net the same margin as in-store, a 30% commission requires roughly a 42.9% menu markup (per Norvet MSP's 2026 analysis).

Ghost kitchens have deflated from their hype. Once projected by Euromonitor to reach a $1 trillion market by 2030, the model has struggled; Deloitte's restaurant lead told CNBC (February 2024) "the impact of ghost kitchens was overestimated." IBISWorld estimated U.S. ghost-kitchen revenue sliding at a 2.2% CAGR to $2.9 billion through 2024, including a 5.2% decline in 2024 alone. CloudKitchens closed facilities and delayed a Middle East IPO in late 2025. Underwriters should treat delivery-only concepts as unproven, thin-collateral credits with high platform dependency.

#### Franchise-sector economics and growth

The IFA's 2025 Franchising Economic Outlook (prepared by FRANdata, released February 5, 2025) projects:

- 851,000 U.S. franchise establishments in 2025 (up about 2.5%, more than 20,000 net units).
- Franchise employment above 9 million (adding roughly 210,000 jobs).
- Franchise output of $936.4 billion (up 4.4% from $896.9 billion in 2024).
- Franchise GDP of $578 billion.
- QSR category growth of about 2.2% in 2025; 21 states raising minimum wages in 2025, pressuring QSR labor.

The subsequent 2026 Franchising Economic Outlook projects franchise output rising from $907.3 billion to $921.4 billion (1.6%) and establishments growing from 832,521 to 845,000 units (a modestly lower base than the 2025 forecast, reflecting FRANdata's data revisions; the base discrepancy should be flagged).

#### Unit-level economics: AUV and buildout

Average unit volume (AUV) and buildout cost vary by segment and brand. AUV is a gross-sales figure disclosed in FDD Item 19, not profit; every brand computes it differently, so footnotes must be read before comparing brands.

- **QSR AUV examples (FDD Item 19 / SEC filings):** Jack in the Box reported system-average gross sales of $1,913,335 for the 12 months ended September 30, 2025 (2026 FDD). Wingstop and Raising Cane's are outliers; Raising Cane's was on pace to eclipse $6 million AUV per QSR Magazine (2024).
- **Fast-casual AUV:** $3 million to $4.5 million on strong brands (Terrapin Construction Group, 2026); Jersey Mike's cited a $1.4 million AUV, 16% store-level margin, and a $515,000 average buildout in its 2026 IPO disclosures (Restaurant Dive).
- **Buildout cost (fast-casual):** $385–$675 per square foot on a 2,200–3,200 sq ft prototype; $14,000–$26,000 per seat ground-up (Terrapin CG, 2026). QSR runs $375–$625 per square foot; full-service casual is highest.
- **FRANdata (2023):** roughly 21% of new franchise concepts were QSRs, with smaller-footprint prototypes pushing initial investment below $500,000.

#### Cap rates for STNL QSR net-lease real estate (a real-estate basis)

STNL QSR properties are valued as real estate on tenant credit and lease term, entirely separate from going-concern operating value. Per The Boulder Group's Q2 2025 Net Lease Research Report (early July 2025) and Q1 2026 report:

- Overall single-tenant net-lease cap rate: 6.79% (up 1 bp quarter-over-quarter, Q2 2025; President Randy Blankstein).
- Retail subsector: 6.57%.
- Corporate QSR credits were the most aggressively priced net-lease assets; the Q1 2026 report cited McDonald's at 4.30%–4.60% (15-year), Chick-fil-A at 4.20%–4.50%, and Wawa at 4.90%–5.20%.
- The market is bifurcated: high-credit tenants trade sub-6%; challenged credits (e.g., Walgreens) exceed 7%.

Lenders must never conflate a 4–5% net-lease cap rate on a corporate-guaranteed ground lease with the going-concern value of an operating franchisee's business, which carries far higher risk and is valued on EBITDA/SDE multiples.

Performance is driven far more by hyperlocal demographics, daypart mix, trade-area density, and competition than by macro real-estate metrics. **Labor-cost driver in focus — California's $20 fast-food wage.** Effective April 1, 2024, AB 1228 raised the minimum wage to $20/hour for fast-food employees at chains with 60+ national locations, a $4 increase (an approximate 8% increase in California's fast-food sector relative to the sector elsewhere in the country, per NBER Working Paper No. 34033) and one of the largest single-industry wage jumps in U.S. history. Evidence on effects is contested and should be presented as such:

- A Harvard Shift Project brief (October 2024) found large wage gains with "no effects on hours, scheduling, or benefits."
- Clemens, Edwards & Meer (NBER Working Paper No. 34033, July 2025, cited in Cato Institute Research Brief No. 458, November 5, 2025, using BLS QCEW data) estimated California fast-food employment declined 2.7% (3.2% adjusting for pre-AB 1228 trends), a median estimate translating to a loss of about 18,000 jobs, and reduced full-service employment 1.55–2.75%.
- CNN Business (May 2, 2025) reported franchisee accounts of cut hours and hiring freezes; one McDonald's franchisee family (24 locations) cut about 170,000 labor hours.

For underwriting, the actionable point is that a QSR pro forma in California must carry a materially higher labor line (a prime-cost floor closer to 58–62% per multi-unit accounting firm TRIS, 2026) than an identical concept in a low-wage state. A single national labor assumption applied across markets is a documented modeling error.

### PART 3 — Layer A: Feasibility & Ramp-Up / Operating Forensics

Documented ways restaurant feasibility and ramp-up forecasts fail lender/SBA underwriting review:

1. **Sales-ramp / AUV assumptions.** New restaurants ramp over 12–24 months to stabilized volume; QSR breakeven often 1–2 years, fast-casual 2–3, full-service 3–5 (Restroworks 2024). A pro forma that assumes mature-unit AUV or FDD Item 19 system-average sales in month one, ignoring the ramp curve, is the most common failure mode. Underwriters should model a graduated ramp and stress the stabilization date.

2. **Prime-cost discipline and cost-inflation sensitivity.** A forecast with prime cost below segment norms (under 55% for FSR, under 52% for QSR) without a documented reason is not credible. Sensitivity to a 100–300 bp food or labor cost increase should be modeled given post-2019 inflation.

3. **The franchised-vs-independent risk divide.** Independents have no proven unit economics; franchised units carry FDD Item 19 financial-performance representations and franchisor training/marketing. But franchise royalties and ad-fund fees (typically 4–8% of sales) plus initial franchise fees compress margins and must be in the model. A franchised pro forma that omits royalties, or an independent pro forma with no comparable benchmark, both fail review.

4. **Site selection and trade-area/daypart errors.** Parsa's OSU research identified location as the number-one failure factor; restaurants in poor locations fail at rates exceeding 80% within three years "regardless of food quality." Daypart concentration (e.g., a breakfast-only or dinner-only concept) and trade-area demographic mismatch are frequent forecast errors.

5. **Competition and cannibalization.** Over-saturation and same-brand encroachment (a franchisor placing a new unit near an existing one) erode AUV. Parsa found restaurant density and ownership turnover strongly correlated (0.9919).

6. **Labor availability and wage-cost underestimation.** Turnover in restaurants is structurally high; management replacement costs run more than $10,000 per manager and more than $16,000 per general manager (Black Box Intelligence, 2025), with hourly replacement around $2,706 (Black Box 2025). Underestimating wage cost in a high-minimum-wage market is a documented killer.

7. **Food-cost / COGS and menu-pricing errors.** Failing to model wholesale volatility (beef, eggs) or to reprice delivery orders erodes margin.

8. **Occupancy-cost ratio.** Rent above roughly 8–10% of sales kills restaurants; "rent is a check that clears when traffic doesn't" (The Level Index). Overpaying for a location is a leading failure mode.

9. **Buildout/FF&E cost overruns.** Ground-up and second-generation buildout costs (see Part 1) frequently run over budget; contingency should be modeled.

10. **Seasonality and daypart concentration.** Tourism-dependent or single-daypart concepts need working capital to bridge troughs.

11. **Delivery / third-party-commission margin erosion.** Blending delivery revenue into total sales without accounting for 15–30% commissions overstates margin.

12. **Working capital and undercapitalization.** Undercapitalization is repeatedly cited as the number-one killer; the cash-flow ramp must be funded through stabilization. SBA now requires a 10% equity injection for startups and changes of ownership (SOP 50 10 8).

13. **DSCR-constrained sizing.** Loan sizing must reflect the ramp; a debt-service schedule that assumes stabilized cash flow from month one will breach DSCR during ramp.

14. **Management/operator experience.** Restaurant experience is critical and SBA weights it heavily; Cornell/Parsa research cited "entrepreneurial incompetence" and underestimation of complexity as major failure contributors. A first-time operator with no industry experience is a material risk flag.

### PART 4 — Layer B: Market Study vs. Feasibility vs. Appraisal, and Form/Program Authority

#### Deliverable distinctions

- **Market study:** assesses demand, trade area, competition, and supportable sales; does not conclude on financial feasibility or value.
- **Feasibility study:** tests whether the projected operation can generate sufficient cash flow to cover operating costs and debt service, incorporating ramp, prime cost, and DSCR; the core lender deliverable for a going-concern credit.
- **Appraisal:** concludes on value. For an operating restaurant, this is a going-concern (business enterprise) value.

#### Going-concern / business enterprise value (central concept)

An operating restaurant is valued as a going concern: the total assets of the business comprising (1) real estate or leasehold, (2) FF&E/equipment, and (3) business/goodwill (including any liquor license and franchise rights). It is typically valued on the income approach (capitalized cash flow) or a multiple of EBITDA/SDE, with an allocation among components. Key lender cautions from appraisal authority (Elliott & Company; Restaurant Analytics; Appraisal Institute):

- Going-concern value can far exceed the underlying real-estate value; in some restaurants real estate is less than half of total value. This is the "kiss of death" risk: on business failure, intangible/goodwill value evaporates and FF&E liquidates at steep discounts, leaving collateral (often real estate reduced ~30% "dark," FF&E reduced ~75%) worth a fraction of the loan.
- Federal appraisal guidance (Interagency Appraisal Guidelines) requires the appraiser to allocate value among real estate, personal property (FF&E), and intangible business value, and to have the training to do so.
- Banks generally cannot lend real-estate dollars against goodwill/intangibles; the SBA guarantee is what allows goodwill to be financed (see below).

**The STNL distinction (critical).** A single-tenant net-lease QSR property (e.g., a corporate-guaranteed McDonald's ground lease) is NOT valued as a going concern. It is real estate valued on the tenant's credit and lease term, at cap rates of roughly 4–7% (Part 1). Do not apply going-concern logic to an STNL investment or net-lease cap rates to an operating franchisee credit.

#### SBA 7(a) and 504

Restaurants, especially franchised QSR, are one of the most common SBA 7(a) uses; the hospitality/food-service sector is the single largest recipient of SBA loans by transaction count (roughly 18–22% of 7(a) approvals in a given year, per Crestmont Capital's reading of SBA data).

- **Why 7(a) dominates over 504 for restaurants:** 7(a) can fund working capital, leasehold improvements, equipment, franchise fees, business acquisition, and goodwill, and restaurant real estate is often leased. 504 is limited to owner-occupied real estate and major fixed assets. PeerSense data shows 504 is used in only about 6% of limited-service and 11% of full-service restaurant SBA loans; 7(a) carries the rest.
- **Loan parameters:** 7(a) maximum $5 million; SBA guarantees 75% (loans over $150,000) to 85% (loans $150,000 and under). Average approved SBA loan sizes: $223,000 for limited-service, $483,000 for full-service (PeerSense, 1992–2025 records).
- **Franchise eligibility and the SBA Franchise Directory.** Reinstated under SOP 50 10 8 effective June 1, 2025. If a brand appears on the Directory, it is eligible; brands that did not execute the new SBA Franchisor Certification by July 31, 2025 were removed. This shifts franchise-eligibility review back to the SBA and streamlines processing for listed brands. Retaining the FDD is now prudent but no longer required; lenders must retain evidence of Directory approval and executed franchise documents.
- **Underwriting tightening under SOP 50 10 8 (effective June 1, 2025):** collateral required for loans above $50,000 (down from $500,000); 10% equity injection for startups and changes of ownership; minimum business credit score raised to 165; IRS tax-transcript verification required.
- **504 for owner-occupied real estate:** minimum 51% owner-occupancy for existing buildings (60% for new construction); structured 50% bank / 40% CDC / 10% borrower equity (higher for startups/special-purpose).
- **Business valuation for change of ownership:** SBA requires an independent business valuation when the amount being financed for goodwill exceeds the SOP threshold; the going-concern/special-purpose nature makes this central to restaurant acquisitions.

#### USDA Business & Industry (B&I) Guaranteed Loan Program (rural restaurants)

Restaurants in eligible rural areas can access USDA B&I financing. Per USDA Rural Development (rd.usda.gov, current as of 2026):

- **"Rural" definition:** areas not in a city or town with population over 50,000 inhabitants (and their contiguous urbanized areas).
- **Maximum loan:** $25 million per borrower standard (up to $40 million for rural cooperatives processing value-added agricultural commodities, with Secretary approval). Note: a "$10 million" figure appears in legacy pre-2020 materials as an administrative approval threshold, not the current statutory cap.
- **Guarantee percentage (FY2026):** 85% for loans under $5 million; 80% for loans of $5 million or more. (Legacy 60/70/80 tiers no longer apply under the OneRD framework, 7 CFR Part 5001, effective October 1, 2020.)
- **Restaurant eligibility:** confirmed. USDA states most retail and service businesses are eligible; a USDA B&I economic assessment submitted to the House Agriculture Committee (September 18, 2025) noted nearly $4 billion invested in accommodations and food services from 2015 to 2024, explicitly including restaurants.
- **Term:** up to 40 years by asset life. Guarantee fee currently 3% (1% for qualifying projects); annual renewal fee 0.55%. Lenders must retain at least 7.5% unguaranteed.
- **Prohibited uses:** lines of credit, owner-occupied/rental housing, golf courses, racetracks/gambling, churches, fraternal organizations, lending/investment/insurance companies, and agricultural production (limited exceptions).

#### Conventional / bank financing

Conventional bank financing is harder for independents to obtain than SBA because of the sector's default profile; equipment financing and franchise-specific lenders fill part of the gap. Franchised concepts with proven unit economics attract more conventional interest. Restaurant real estate that is owner-occupied and general-purpose is more bankable than special-purpose buildout.

#### Bridge / hard money / equipment / alternative

Equipment financing is common for FF&E. Bridge and hard-money loans carry higher rates and short terms. **Merchant cash advances (MCA) should be flagged as high-cost, high-risk:** alternative lenders and MCA providers show default rates of 5–15%+ (Crestmont Capital), and fixed daily repayment is especially dangerous for a seasonal, thin-margin restaurant. MCA stacking is a documented driver of restaurant distress and should be a red flag in any refinance review.

#### Appraisal standards and environmental note

The going-concern appraisal must allocate value among real estate/leasehold, FF&E, and business/goodwill, and disclose the effect of FF&E and business enterprise value on the concluded value (Interagency Appraisal Guidelines; USPAP). Leasehold-improvement value is a distinct consideration for leased space. For owned real estate, a Phase I Environmental Site Assessment is typically required by the lender/SBA, but the feasibility-study author does NOT itself perform Phase I/II ESA work; that is a separate environmental-professional engagement.

### PART 5 — Supporting Authority

#### The real data on failure rates (nuanced, contested)

As detailed in Part 1: Parsa et al. (Cornell, 2005) found 26.16% first-year failure for independents, cumulative three-year closure of roughly 57–62%, and only a marginal franchise/independent gap. The "roughly 60% close within 3 years and ~80% within 5 years" framing appears in some industry summaries (e.g., a Forbes-cited restaurant-failure analysis), but the underlying academic data is single-market and methodology-dependent, and "closure" conflates financial failure with voluntary exit. Lenders should treat any single failure-rate figure with skepticism and underwrite to cash-flow feasibility, not to a myth.

#### The franchise model as risk mitigation (modest, mixed evidence)

The franchise model provides FDD Item 19 benchmarks, training, brand marketing, and proven systems, which support underwriting confidence. But the survival advantage over independents is modest, not dramatic:

- Parsa (2005): marginal difference (57.2% franchise vs. 61.4% independent three-year closure).
- SBA loan-performance data is mixed: franchise SBA 7(a) loans have at times defaulted slightly above the all-industry average (a FRANdata observation that franchisees historically ran roughly 1.5 points higher default, attributed to smaller loan sizes), while the most-financed top franchise brands default well below average.
- High "85–92% of franchises survive five years" claims circulate in industry/marketing content but are not well-substantiated academically and should be flagged as unverified.

The defensible underwriting position: franchising materially reduces information risk (you can benchmark AUV and cost structure against the FDD and system peers) and provides operational support, but it does not eliminate unit-level failure risk, and royalties compress margin. Franchise status is a positive underwriting factor, not a guarantee.

#### The labor/wage story

Minimum-wage escalation is the defining cost trend. California's $20 fast-food wage (April 2024) is the template; 21 states raised minimum wages in 2025 (FRANdata). Tipped-wage debates continue in full-service. Automation and self-order kiosks are the operator response: the NRA reported a meaningful share of operators planning automated labor-management and kiosk adoption. For underwriting, the labor line must be localized to the specific market's wage floor and trend.

#### Food-cost inflation

Food-away-from-home menu inflation ran 4.1% (2024) and 3.8% (2025) and is forecast at about 3.6% (2026), all above the ~3.5% historical average (USDA ERS). Wholesale inputs remain volatile (beef and eggs). Operators have managed food-cost ratios more through menu engineering and pricing than through wholesale relief (NRA).

#### Off-premise / delivery structural shift

Off-premise is now a larger revenue share than 2019 for about half of operators (NRA), but carries 15–30% commission drag on third-party channels. First-party ordering and loyalty are the margin-defense strategies. Ghost kitchens have contracted (Part 1).

#### Consumer spending and the value barbell

Consumers are value-seeking and trading down; lower-income households cut restaurant visits first (Black Box found restaurants in the lowest-income ZIP codes posted negative same-store sales and softest traffic in Q1 2025). Higher-income diners trade down from fine dining to casual. The result is a barbell: value QSR/fast-casual and premium experiences outperform the squeezed middle. Post-COVID recovery is complete on dollars but not on traffic, and 2025's growth leaned on easy year-over-year comparisons that will not repeat, per Black Box's forward guidance.

#### Buildout / replacement-value context

Buildout cost ranks QSR (roughly $375–$625/sq ft) below fast-casual ($385–$675/sq ft, $14,000–$26,000/seat ground-up) below full-service casual (highest), per Terrapin Construction Group (2026). New franchise concepts increasingly use smaller footprints to hold initial investment below $500,000 (FRANdata 2023). Replacement-value and buildout context matter for both the cost approach in appraisal and the FF&E collateral analysis.

## Recommendations

**Stage 1 — Concept and sponsor screen (go/no-go before spending on a full feasibility study).**
- Classify the deal by segment (FSR / QSR / fast-casual / ghost kitchen) and by franchised vs. independent. This determines everything downstream.
- For franchised deals, confirm the brand is on the SBA Franchise Directory and obtain FDD Item 19. For independents, require comparable market benchmarks.
- Require documented operator restaurant experience. First-time operator with no industry experience: escalate or decline.
- *Threshold that changes the call:* franchised, Directory-listed, experienced operator = proceed to Stage 2 with standard scrutiny; independent startup with inexperienced operator = require substantially higher equity, stronger secondary collateral, or decline.

**Stage 2 — Feasibility and pro forma stress test.**
- Model a graduated 12–24 month sales ramp; do NOT accept mature-unit AUV or FDD system-average sales from month one. Benchmark stabilized AUV against FDD Item 19 (franchised) or trade-area comparables (independent).
- Verify prime cost is within segment norms: FSR 60–65%, QSR 55–60%, fast-casual in between. Reject forecasts below 52–55% without documented justification.
- Localize the labor line to the market's minimum wage. In California and other high-wage markets, use a prime-cost floor of roughly 58–62% for QSR.
- Confirm occupancy cost stays under 8–10% of sales (target under 7% in urban markets).
- Include franchise royalties/ad fees (4–8%) and delivery commissions (15–30% on off-premise) in the model.
- *Thresholds that change the call:* stabilized DSCR under 1.25x on stressed assumptions, or prime cost above 65%, or occupancy above 10% = restructure (lower leverage, more equity, rent renegotiation) or decline.

**Stage 3 — Capital structure, liquidity, and sizing.**
- Require the SBA-mandated 10% minimum equity injection (SOP 50 10 8) for startups and acquisitions; consider more for independents.
- Size a working-capital / liquidity reserve sufficient to fund the cash-flow ramp through stabilization (commonly 6–12 months of operating shortfall plus debt service). Undercapitalization is the number-one killer.
- For acquisitions with goodwill, obtain an independent going-concern business valuation and confirm the goodwill allocation is SBA-financeable.
- Flag any MCA/stacked short-term debt as a distress signal; prefer refinancing into term structure.

**Stage 4 — Collateral, appraisal, and program fit.**
- Order the correct deliverable: going-concern appraisal for an operating restaurant (with allocation among real estate, FF&E, goodwill); STNL real-estate appraisal for a net-lease investment. Never conflate the two bases.
- Recognize the "kiss of death" risk: if going-concern value is disproportionately goodwill/intangible, treat high-LTV lending with caution and weight real-estate and FF&E liquidation value.
- Match the program: 7(a) for leased space, working capital, equipment, franchise fees, and goodwill; 504 for owner-occupied general-purpose real estate; USDA B&I for eligible rural (population under 50,000) restaurants (85% guarantee under $5M, up to $25M).
- Require Phase I ESA for owned real estate via a qualified environmental professional (not the feasibility author).

**Benchmarks that change the call, summarized:** franchised + Directory-listed + experienced operator (favorable); prime cost under 60–65% by segment (favorable); occupancy under 8% (favorable); stabilized DSCR 1.25x+ on stressed ramp (favorable); working-capital reserve covering ramp to breakeven (required); AUV forecast within FDD Item 19 middle tier (favorable). Breaches move the deal to restructure or decline.

## Caveats

- **Provider divergence on industry size and traffic.** The National Restaurant Association, Technomic, Circana (formerly NPD Group), and Black Box Intelligence use different definitions, samples, and methodologies. NRA's 2025 report adopted a new USDA-Food-Expenditure-Series methodology, breaking comparability with prior years. Establishment counts range from ~720,000 (NAICS 722 payroll establishments) to over 1 million (broad outlet definition). Do not blend bases.
- **Failure-rate data is genuinely contested and methodology-dependent.** The Parsa (Cornell, 2005) study is single-market (Columbus, Ohio, 1996–1999) and defines "failure" as ownership turnover/closure, not bankruptcy. First-year figures range from about 17% (Luo-Stark; BLS) to 26% (Parsa) to the debunked 90% myth. Cumulative multi-year closure estimates (57–80% by five years) vary widely. Treat all single figures skeptically; this is the most important caveat in the monitor.
- **Franchise-vs-independent survival advantage is modest and mixed, not dramatic.** Academic evidence (Parsa) shows only a marginal gap; SBA default data is mixed. High "85–92% five-year franchise survival" claims are industry/marketing-sourced and unverified.
- **Same-store sales vs. traffic vs. total sales are different bases.** Black Box same-store figures are chain-weighted and exclude most independents. Growth in 2025 is price/mix-led; a positive same-store sales number can mask negative traffic.
- **Forecast vs. actual flags.** NRA's $1.5 trillion 2025 sales and 15.9 million employment are projections; IFA/FRANdata franchise figures are forecasts; Black Box's Q4 2025 / 2026 softening outlook is a projection. The IFA 2025 and 2026 outlooks show different establishment bases (851,000 vs. 845,000) reflecting data revisions.
- **Single-provider flags.** SBA default rates by NAICS (19.8% limited-service; 4.4% full-service) come from the PeerSense database (a single commercial aggregator of SBA records); the SBA does not publish a unified default table by NAICS, so treat as directional. STNL cap rates are from a single provider (The Boulder Group). AUV/buildout figures are brand-disclosed (FDD Item 19) and computed inconsistently across brands.
- **California wage-effect evidence is contested.** The Harvard Shift Project (no adverse employment effect) and Clemens/Edwards/Meer via Cato Institute (roughly 18,000 jobs lost) reach opposite conclusions; both are cited to avoid single-source bias.
- **Ghost-kitchen market-size estimates diverge wildly** (IBISWorld's ~$2.9 billion U.S. figure vs. multi-hundred-billion-dollar global projections from other firms); the segment is contracting in the U.S. and should be treated as unproven.
- **USDA B&I parameters change annually** via Federal Register notice; the 85%/80% guarantee split and fees are FY2026 figures and should be reverified at application.
- **Prospective/discontinued items.** The SBA Franchise Directory was discontinued in 2023 and reinstated June 1, 2025; confirm current Directory status at underwriting. Dun & Bradstreet's business-failure series (cited in older failure-rate literature) ceased publication in 1998.
