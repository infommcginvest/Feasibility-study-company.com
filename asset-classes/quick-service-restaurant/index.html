The QSR Thesis

A QSR franchise is a business, not a building.

A quick-service restaurant unit is a going-concern foodservice operating business valued on cash flow and business-enterprise value, not passive real estate, and the organizing lens is the franchise model, franchisee-versus-franchisor economics. The franchisee operates under a brand governed by a Franchise Disclosure Document, paying an initial franchise fee commonly $25,000 to $50,000, an ongoing royalty of roughly 4 to 6 percent of sales, and an ad-fund contribution of roughly 2 to 4 percent. That 6-to-10-percent-plus load comes off the top, compressing a four-wall margin already pressured by prime cost, food plus labor, of roughly 55 to 65 percent of sales.727 We prepare the market study, the feasibility study, and the going-concern appraisal input a QSR file needs, aligned to the standard that will judge it.

The critical distinction lenders miss is that the operating franchise and the underlying real estate are two different assets with two different values. QSR is the premier net-lease and single-tenant (STNL) tenant category, but that real-estate value is separate from the operating business. Per the Boulder Group's Q1 2026 report, a McDonald's ground lease trades at 4.40 percent and a Chick-fil-A ground lease at 4.50 percent, all corporate QSR at 5.82 percent, and all franchisee QSR at 6.80 percent, a roughly 98-basis-point spread that prices the difference between franchisor covenant and franchisee operating credit.6 The same physical drive-thru can carry a going-concern value on its cash flow and a very different real-estate value on its lease.

Two forces are institutionalizing the borrower base. Consolidation into large multi-unit operators and private-equity platforms is the dominant one, Flynn Group, the world's largest franchisee, now runs roughly 2,745 units on about $4.5 billion in sales,1112 and SBA is the dominant financing channel, with the Franchise Directory reinstated under SOP 50 10 8.22 What follows is organized as a working desk: a national and site-feasibility monitor, the ramp and Item 19 failure forensics that sink QSR studies, the capital-source routing that decides which deliverable a project needs, and the study-type distinctions competitors state loosely. Every figure is dated and attributed in the sources below.

The Unit-Economics & Site Monitor

Where QSR feasibility stands, market type by market type.

A feasibility read for the major U.S. market types, organized by the variables that decide a QSR site: traffic and daytime population, drive-thru suitability and site availability, competition and saturation, and the labor and minimum-wage environment. Metro-level QSR unit economics are largely proprietary, so this is a framework, not a dataset; treat single-provider figures as flagged.

The national picture frames every site. The U.S. fast-food category is large but measured inconsistently: IBISWorld puts the Fast Food Restaurants industry at $416.0 billion in 2026 across roughly 223,000 businesses, while narrower QSR-market research clusters near $301 billion for 2025 and one estimate lands at $195.3 billion for 2024 on a different basis, a data-quality divergence to flag rather than reconcile.12 At the umbrella level the National Restaurant Association projects total restaurant and foodservice sales of $1.5 trillion in 2025 and $1.55 trillion in 2026, and there are more than 200,000 QSR locations among roughly 691,181 chain restaurant locations counted in 2024.3128 Food-away-from-home spending reached a record 55.1 percent of food spending in 2023 and held near 55 percent in 2024, and the chained or franchised structure captured about 52.34 percent of the QSR market in 2025.164 Off-premise formats now account for more than 70 percent of revenue at leading brands, with drive-thru's share of QSR orders easing from 83 percent in 2020 to about 65 percent in 2025 as dine-in and delivery recovered.45 The result is a resilient, structurally franchised category whose site-level feasibility turns entirely on local traffic, drive-thru suitability, saturation, and the wage environment.

AUV is a revenue number, not profit, and it diverges wildly by brand

No figure in QSR is more misread than average unit volume. The QSR 50, on year-end 2023 data, put Chick-fil-A near $7.45 million, Raising Cane's near $5.69 million, McDonald's near $4.0 million, Whataburger near $3.96 million, Culver's near $3.49 million, Taco Bell near $2.1 million, and Wendy's near $2.06 million, while Subway's AUV sits near $490,000.820 McDonald's 2024 FDD reported average annual sales of $3,966,000 for franchised traditional restaurants against a median of $3,797,000, Chick-fil-A reported a 2025 median freestanding AUV of $9,087,673, and Wingstop grew system-wide sales 32 percent year over year with AUV above $6.5 million across 828 units and 72 percent of orders digital.91021 But AUV is revenue, not profit: a $3 million unit at a 6 percent margin ($180,000) is less profitable than a $1.2 million unit at a 20 percent margin ($240,000). AUV must be paired with margin analysis, never read alone.

The franchise load comes off the top

A QSR franchisee's P&L carries three franchise-specific costs above operating expense. Royalties run roughly 4 to 6 percent of gross sales, McDonald's charges a 4 percent service fee (moving new U.S. and Canada units to 5 percent from 2024), Burger King 4.5 percent, Wendy's 4 percent, Popeyes 5 percent, KFC 5 percent, Taco Bell 5.5 percent, Wingstop 6 percent, and Subway 8 percent, the highest in mainstream QSR.79 Ad-fund contributions add roughly 2 to 4 percent, taking McDonald's combined load to 8 percent, among the highest in major QSR, and Subway's total royalty-plus-ad to 12.5 percent, described as punishing against a sub-$500,000 AUV.19 Initial franchise fees commonly run $25,000 to $50,000, McDonald's $45,000, Wendy's $40,000, Taco Bell $25,000 to $45,000, Subway $15,000, and Chick-fil-A $10,000.920 Agreement terms are long, McDonald's traditional term is generally 20 years with no right to renew, and Wendy's current agreement provides a 20-year term with a 10-year renewal.918 Underneath the load, prime cost runs roughly 55 to 65 percent of sales, four-wall EBITDA lands in the 12-to-30-percent range for well-run units, a 6 percent royalty alone reduces franchisee cash earnings by 15 to 25 percent, and all-in development for an 1,800-square-foot freestanding drive-thru unit runs roughly $1.3 to $2.5 million, or about $220 to $580 per square foot, before land.27726

Consolidation, net-lease pricing, and the guarantor split

The borrower base is institutionalizing. Multi-unit operators now control about 54 percent of U.S. franchised units, and Flynn Group, the world's largest franchisee, grew from 2,520 units in 2024 to 2,745 in 2025, with about $4.5 billion in annual sales and roughly 75,000 employees across seven brands, a scale built on the transformative 2021 acquisition of the bankrupt NPC International (937 Pizza Hut and 194 Wendy's units), the largest franchisee-to-franchisee deal in U.S. history at the time.11128 Billion-dollar operators Dhanani Group (~$1.732 billion), Sun Holdings (~$1.59 billion), and KBP Brands (~$1.436 billion) sit behind it, and Restaurant Brands International bought Carrols (1,023 Burger King and 59 Popeyes units) for about $1.0 billion in May 2024, committing a further $500 million to reimage more than 600 restaurants.1713 Multi-unit platforms of five-plus units trade near 5 to 7x EBITDA and 20-plus-unit Wingstop platforms at 7 to 8x, PE roll-ups buy near 5.5x and exit at 7 to 9x, and Roark Capital's franchisor-level Subway acquisition closed April 30, 2024 near $9.6 billion, about 10 to 12x EBITDA.728 On the real-estate basis, QSR is the tightest-priced net-lease retail category, with McDonald's ground leases at 4.40 percent, Chick-fil-A at 4.50 percent, all corporate QSR at 5.82 percent, and franchisee QSR at 6.80 percent, a spread that is purely a function of the guarantor.6

Common Review Failures

How QSR feasibility and ramp forecasts fail review.

Sales ramp, the franchise load, and labor are the variables a credit committee scrutinizes most, and the places QSR studies most often break. Each failure below is tied to a real mechanism or number.

  1. Day-one AUV instead of a ramp curve

    A new unit typically ramps over one to three years and rarely opens at the Item 19 system average. Assuming day-one AUV at or above the disclosed average is the most common feasibility error; the fix is to underwrite an explicit ramp curve and stress the trough year against debt service.7

  2. Mis-reading FDD Item 19

    Item 19 is a franchisor-reported, voluntary, survivorship-biased representation of open units, not a guarantee under the FTC Franchise Rule. Read median against average, McDonald's 2024 median was $3,797,000 versus a $3,966,000 average, examine top versus bottom quartile, and treat a brand with no Item 19 disclosure, such as Subway, as a flag in itself.919

  3. Understating the royalty and ad-fund load

    Mis-modeling the 6-to-10-percent-plus royalty-and-ad load produces an overstated four-wall margin. After royalty of 4 to 6 percent, an ad fund of 2 to 4 percent, and rent, a thin margin can evaporate; a 6 percent royalty alone cuts franchisee cash earnings 15 to 25 percent. Model the load explicitly, off the top.7

  4. Labor and wage risk, the number-one issue

    Labor runs roughly 25 to 35 percent of sales, and minimum-wage increases hit it directly, above all California's $20 fast-food floor under AB 1228, effective April 2024. One California McDonald's franchisee family reported cutting about 170,000 labor hours in response. The employment effect is genuinely contested, UC Berkeley's IRLE found no employment loss and price increases near 1.5 percent, while an NBER working paper estimated about 18,000 lost California fast-food jobs, so present it as competing evidence and underwrite the wage floor and staffing plan directly.141528

  5. Third-party-delivery margin dilution

    Delivery commissions of 15 to 30 percent, with effective all-in costs of 30 to 40 percent, can erase a QSR's thin margin on the delivery channel. A heavy delivery-versus-dine-in mix without a menu-price offset destroys unit profitability; confirm the delivery mix and pricing strategy.4

  6. Drive-thru throughput and site-suitability errors

    Drive-thru accounts for more than 70 percent of sales at leading brands, so a site with poor stacking, access, or lane configuration caps throughput and volume, a single lane clears about 120 cars per hour against 180-plus for a dual lane. Circulation and stacking capacity are feasibility-critical, not cosmetic.427

  7. Territory, encroachment, and franchisor health

    Non-exclusive territories invite cannibalization, Subway's are cited as a driver, and even McDonald's reportedly caps operator volume by opening nearby units as a store nears $6 million. Franchisor deterioration is a live risk: Subway posted multiple years of net unit closures. Confirm territory and encroachment protection in the agreement and read the Item 20 unit-count trend.1921

Study Types

Market study, feasibility study, appraisal: three questions.

These three documents answer different questions and are not substitutes. Borrowers and brokers conflate them constantly; SBA lenders and credit committees do not.

What each document answers, and the standard that governs it.
DocumentQuestion answeredGoverning standard
AppraisalWhat is it worth? A going-concern / business-enterprise value via the income approach (EBITDA or SDE multiple), allocated to real property, FF&E, and franchise intangible.USPAP
Market studyIs there demand? Trade area, traffic and daytime population, the competitive set, saturation, and drive-thru suitability.Trade-area demand analysis
Feasibility studyDoes it pencil? Projected AUV and ramp, prime cost, the franchise load, four-wall and franchisee-net economics, and debt-service coverage.Lender underwriting + FDD Item 19 read

The distinction that governs a QSR file is that the operating unit and the real estate are two different assets. A going-concern QSR is valued as a business through the income approach on cash flow, an EBITDA or SDE multiple, with the total business value allocated among the real property or leasehold, the equipment and FF&E, and the intangible business and franchise value, the franchise agreement and brand. That is fundamentally different from a single-tenant net-leased (STNL) QSR, a ground-leased or NNN drive-thru pad, which is valued as real estate on the tenant and guarantor credit and the lease, at a cap rate. The same physical building can carry two very different values depending on whether it is analyzed as an operating going concern or a leased real-estate asset, and whether the lease is corporate-guaranteed or franchisee-guaranteed. Single-unit owner-operator deals price on SDE, multi-unit groups on EBITDA, and net-leased pads on a cap rate, three bases that must never be conflated.

One scope boundary is worth stating. QSR is generally a low-environmental-risk use, but a lender will typically expect a standard Phase I Environmental Site Assessment, with attention to prior site uses such as gas stations or dry cleaners and to grease and underground issues. The feasibility or market-study author does not perform the Phase I or II ESA; that is a separate environmental professional's engagement. The market and feasibility work projects AUV and ramp, models the franchise load and four-wall and franchisee-net economics, and sizes debt service through the trough; it does not opine on environmental condition.

QSR Questions

Quick-service restaurant feasibility and market-study questions.

What is the difference between a QSR market study and a feasibility study?

A market study assesses demand for a site: trade area, traffic and daytime population, the competitive set, QSR saturation, and drive-thru suitability. A feasibility study goes further, testing whether a specific franchise unit pencils, projected average unit volume (AUV) and a one-to-three-year ramp, prime cost, the franchise royalty and ad-fund load, four-wall and franchisee-net economics, and debt-service coverage through the ramp trough. It answers whether the deal covers its debt for this lender. Because a QSR franchise is a going-concern operating business rather than passive real estate, the feasibility work centers on cash flow, not on the building alone.

Is a QSR franchise valued as a business or as real estate?

Both, but on two different bases that must never be conflated. The operating franchise unit is valued as a going concern through the income approach on cash flow (an EBITDA or SDE multiple), with the total business-enterprise value allocated among the real property or leasehold, the equipment and FF&E, and the intangible franchise and brand value. A single-tenant net-leased (STNL) drive-thru pad is valued instead as real estate, at a cap rate on the tenant and guarantor credit and the lease. The same physical building can carry two very different values depending on whether it is analyzed as an operating going concern or as a leased real-estate asset, and whether the lease is corporate-guaranteed or franchisee-guaranteed.

Can a QSR franchise be financed with an SBA loan?

Yes. QSR franchises are among the most common SBA 7(a) uses, and because QSR real estate is often leased, 7(a) is the dominant vehicle for the franchise fee, buildout, equipment, and working capital, with 504 used for owner-occupied real estate under the 51 percent existing and 60 percent new-construction occupancy tests. Eligibility runs through the SBA Franchise Directory: under SOP 50 10 8, effective June 1, 2025, the SBA reinstated the Directory, and a brand must be listed, with the franchisor having executed the new SBA Franchisor Certification, for streamlined eligibility. Multi-brand franchisees must clear eligibility for each brand independently, and change-of-ownership deals require a business valuation and a going-concern appraisal.

What is FDD Item 19, and why does it matter to lenders?

Item 19 of the Franchise Disclosure Document is the franchisor's voluntary financial performance representation, and Item 7 is the estimated initial investment. Item 19 is the starting point for a revenue assumption, but it is voluntary under the FTC Franchise Rule, survivorship-biased because it reflects only open units, and expressly not a guarantee. Average, median, and top-quartile figures differ materially: McDonald's 2024 disclosure reported average annual sales of $3,966,000 against a median of $3,797,000, and some brands, notably Subway, make no Item 19 disclosure at all, which is itself a flag. A defensible underwriting AUV is tied to the specific site, not to the system average.

How much does it cost to open a QSR, and what is the franchise load?

All-in development for an 1,800-square-foot freestanding QSR with a drive-thru runs roughly $1.3 million to $2.5 million, with ground-up prototypes at about $220 to $580 per square foot depending on metro, before land. On top of operating costs, the franchisee P&L carries three franchise-specific charges: an initial franchise fee commonly $25,000 to $50,000, an ongoing royalty of roughly 4 to 6 percent of sales, and an ad-fund contribution of roughly 2 to 4 percent. That combined 6-to-10-percent-plus load comes off the top, ahead of rent, and compresses a four-wall margin already pressured by prime cost, food plus labor, of roughly 55 to 65 percent of sales.

Which QSR markets are most favorable for new units?

Sun Belt suburban markets, Texas, Florida, Georgia, Arizona, and North Carolina, are the primary QSR growth corridor: car-centric demographics favor drive-thru pad development, pad sites are available, and construction cost runs below the national average, with Texas roughly 5 to 15 percent below. High-cost coastal metros carry a structurally higher labor-cost floor, epitomized by California's $20 fast-food minimum wage, and scarce, expensive drive-thru pads. Midwest markets are mature and largely replacement-driven, the Mountain West offers growth with above-average construction cost, and rural sites offer easy drive-thru development and USDA Business and Industry eligibility but depend on capturing highway and daytime traffic.

What cap rate does net-leased QSR real estate trade at?

QSR is the tightest-priced net-lease retail category, and it splits sharply by guarantor credit. Per the Boulder Group's Q1 2026 Net Lease Research Report, a McDonald's ground lease traded at 4.40 percent and a Chick-fil-A ground lease at 4.50 percent, all corporate-guaranteed QSR at 5.82 percent, and all franchisee-guaranteed QSR at 6.80 percent, a roughly 98-basis-point headline spread that reflects the difference between franchisor covenant and franchisee operating credit. Shorter-term, non-rated restaurant tenants can trade 200 to 300 basis points wider. For a lender financing the real estate rather than the operating business, that guarantor distinction is the core underwriting variable.
