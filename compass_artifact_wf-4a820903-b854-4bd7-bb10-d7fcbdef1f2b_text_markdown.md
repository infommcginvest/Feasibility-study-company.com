# North Carolina Feasibility-Study Research Desk: The Oversupply & Pipeline Monitor

*Data current to Q2 2026. Every figure carries a named source and date. Unverifiable claims are flagged "FLAG:" rather than asserted.*

## TL;DR

- **A single statewide North Carolina feasibility assumption is indefensible in 2026.** The state splits into at least four economies moving in opposite directions: the booming **Research Triangle** (Raleigh-Durham-Cary — the #1 US biomanufacturing cluster per NCBiotech, plus tech and life science); **Charlotte** (the nation's #2 banking center, fast-growing but digesting a record apartment wave); the slower **Piedmont Triad** (Greensboro-Winston-Salem-High Point, now transformed by Toyota's $13.9B battery megaplant); a **vast rural east** (agriculture, hogs, poultry — much flat or declining); and the **western mountains** devastated by Hurricane Helene (Sept 27, 2024). Same-asset divergence is concrete: per Northmarq's Q3 2025 data, **Charlotte multifamily asking rents were down 1.3% YoY** with vacancy up 50 bps to 8.2% on record deliveries, and Raleigh-Durham vacancy was tracking ~8.0%, while **Durham-Chapel Hill posted a 14.8% median-rent jump** (Realtor.com via Unicorn Rentals) and Asheville faces a worsened housing shortage post-Helene.
- **The single most valuable competitor-absent deliverable is a metro×asset Oversupply Monitor paired with a funding-routing map.** No competing feasibility shop publishes a defensible per-cell supply-pressure read (OVERSUPPLIED / BALANCED / UNDERSUPPLIED / DIGESTING) for NC's metros, nor maps which of SBA 7(a), SBA 504, USDA B&I/REAP, or conventional/CMBS capital gravitates to which asset and geography — anchored on the fact that the **national #1 SBA 7(a) lender by dollar volume, Live Oak Bank, is headquartered in Wilmington, NC.**
- **On Certificate of Need, NC is one of the strictest, broadest FULL-CON states — but with reform and live constitutional litigation.** CON is administered by the NC DHHS Division of Health Service Regulation under NCGS Chapter 131E, Article 9, covering hospital/nursing/adult-care beds, ASCs, major medical equipment (MRI/CT/PET/linear accelerators), home health, hospice, and dialysis. **HB 76 (Session Law 2023-7)** raised thresholds and phases in ASC/equipment exemptions in more populous counties. In **Singleton v. NCDHHS**, a Wake County three-judge panel ruled **unanimously against** the challenger in December 2025; an appeal back toward the NC Supreme Court is expected. Net read matches New York/Illinois — full CON, low healthcare oversupply risk, CON as a gating barrier — but with reform/litigation loosening risk to track. **Competitors stating "NC has no CON" are catastrophically wrong; those citing only pre-2023 rules miss HB 76 and Singleton.**
- **The signature underwriting error is climate/insurance mispricing — especially inland flood.** Helene proved a "low-risk" mountain market can suffer catastrophic flooding; per NC Newsline (Apr 22, 2025), only **~5.2% of homes and ~10.2% of commercial properties** in the affected region carried FEMA flood insurance (Buncombe County had ~1,295 NFIP policies), and NC OSBM's December 2024 Damage and Needs Assessment put total damage/needs at **$59.6 billion**. Combined with coastal Beach Plan exposure and the 2025 homeowners rate settlement (7.5%+7.5%), insurance is the differentiator competitors ignore.

## Key Findings

1. **Charlotte AND Raleigh-Durham multifamily are both in an oversupply/digestion cycle — the classic Sun Belt failure trap.** Charlotte delivered a record 16,700+ units in 2024 (MMG, Jan 2025) and 16,200+ more through Nov 2025 (Yardi Matrix, Jan 2026); asking rents were **down 1.3% YoY** with vacancy at **8.2% in Q3 2025** (Northmarq). Raleigh-Durham pushed vacancy above 11% at the peak of its largest-ever supply wave (APG/Yardi), with rents down ~0.7% YoY late 2025 (Yardi Matrix, Feb 2026). Underwriting 2021-era rent growth here is indefensible; both markets are DIGESTING, tipping toward BALANCED in 2026 as deliveries fall 60%+ (Northmarq Q1 2026).

2. **The Triangle is simultaneously the #1 US biomanufacturing boom AND a lab-space oversupply story.** North Carolina booked over $10 billion in life-science investment in 2024 (NCBiotech); Fujifilm Biotechnologies opened its **$3.2 billion** Holly Springs plant (Sept 24, 2025) and Novo Nordisk announced a **$4.1 billion** Clayton expansion. Yet Triangle lab vacancy rose to **14.3% in Q2 2024** from 8.7% a year earlier (CBRE), and one 2026 tracker put Research Triangle lab vacancy at **11.3%** (Buildermuse, Q1 2026) — far healthier than Boston/SF/San Diego (18–30%+) but a clear correction. Purpose-built biomanufacturing (owner-occupied, credit-tenant) is UNDERSUPPLIED/BALANCED; speculative multi-tenant lab is DIGESTING.

3. **Industrial is NC's signature growth engine, led by megaprojects — but with megaproject-timing risk.** Toyota's **$13.9 billion** Liberty battery plant (Randolph County) began production in 2025 and targets **5,100 jobs** (NC Commerce, Nov 2025). But **Wolfspeed filed Chapter 11 on June 30, 2025** (~$6.5B debt), and **VinFast delayed its Chatham County EV plant to 2028** (Carolina Journal). Charlotte industrial vacancy improved to **7.3% in Q1 2026** (CBRE) after peaking at a 10-year high; the Triad is stacking build-to-suit wins. Sizing workforce housing to peak projected employment before it materializes — or when a project fails — is the trap.

4. **North Carolina is one of the fastest-growing large states, but growth is spatially concentrated.** Census Vintage 2025 estimates put NC at **11,197,968 as of July 1, 2025** (NC OSBM/State Demographer), a **+145,907 (1.3%) YoY gain** that ranked NC 3rd nationally in growth rate, with NC leading all states in net domestic migration (~+84,000). The state is projected to become the 7th-largest by the early 2030s (NC OSBM). Charlotte and Raleigh added the most residents; Wilmington/coastal counties (Brunswick was 6th-fastest in the US) and Triangle suburbs (Johnston, Iredell) boomed, while rural northeastern counties and Helene-hit western counties lag or lose population. A statewide capture rate blends booming and shrinking geographies into a meaningless average.

5. **Healthcare supply is heavily gated by strict CON, sharply limiting oversupply risk in CON-covered assets.** NC's full CON regime caps nursing-home/adult-care beds, ASCs, and major equipment; the AFP Foundation counts ~$4.2B in denied CON investment (2012–Feb 2025) and NC has among the lowest inpatient-rehab bed ratios nationally (0.38 vs. 0.65 US per 1,000 age 65+, KeyBanc May 2025). This makes CON-covered healthcare a low-oversupply, high-barrier feasibility environment — but HB 76 reforms and the Singleton litigation inject loosening/uncertainty.

#### Multifamily

**Charlotte** is a signature Sun Belt oversupply/digestion story. Developers added a record **16,700+ units in 2024**, a 25% jump over 2023 and more than double the 2015–2019 average of 7,400 (MMG Real Estate Advisors, 2025 Charlotte Forecast). More than **16,200 units delivered through November 2025** (6.6% of stock, vs. 2.8% nationally), with **~27,000 units under construction and ~92,000 in planning** (Yardi Matrix, Jan 2026). Rents were **down 1.3% YoY** as of Q3 2025 with **vacancy rising 50 bps to 8.2%** (Northmarq, Q3 2025); Yardi's trailing-3-month asking rent slipped 0.3% to $1,578 through November 2025 with occupancy at 94.2%. Concessions ran on ~27% of units (MMG). South End posted the strongest absorption. Read: **DIGESTING**, flipping toward BALANCED in 2026 as starts fell ~40% YoY.

**Raleigh-Durham (the Triangle)** shows strong tech/life-science demand colliding with a record supply wave. ~14,500 units delivered in 2024; **10,899 delivered in 2025 with ~11,854 underway** as of December (Yardi Matrix, Feb 2026). Vacancy sat at **7.7–8.0%** through 2025 (Northmarq), and one sub-market tracker put Durham vacancy at **12.25% in Q1 2025** (Lee & Associates). Rents dipped ~0.7% YoY late 2025 (Yardi). But the pipeline is collapsing — **deliveries down 63% YoY in Q1 2026** (Northmarq) — and Durham-Chapel Hill posted a 14.8% median-rent jump (Realtor.com via Unicorn Rentals). Read: **DIGESTING**, first vacancy improvement since 2021 expected in 2026.

**Greensboro/Winston-Salem/High Point (Piedmont Triad)** is more balanced; average apartment rents near $1,320 (Oct 2025, RentCafe) are well below Charlotte, with limited high-rise luxury supply and Toyota-driven demand. Read: **BALANCED**.

**Asheville (western)** faces a housing shortage worsened by Helene, which removed housing stock and displaced residents. Read: **UNDERSUPPLIED** on fundamentals, though demand is complicated by displacement and slow recovery.

**Wilmington** (coastal growth; metro was the 7th-fastest-growing US metro by percentage 2024–2025, Census) and **Fayetteville** (Fort Bragg/Fort Liberty) round out the secondary markets. Reads: Wilmington **BALANCED**, Fayetteville **BALANCED**.

#### Self-Storage

North Carolina's per-capita storage supply now **exceeds 10 sf per capita** statewide (StorageCafe/Yardi Matrix, 2025), above the ~7.8 national average (Yardi Matrix, Dec 2025). Raleigh sits at **7.6 sf/capita** across 81 facilities (StorageCafe, 2025); Charlotte at ~7.1 sf/capita (StorageCafe/Yardi). Greensboro (10.7 sf/capita) and Fayetteville (11 sf/capita) are more heavily supplied. NC delivered ~2.7 million sf in 2025 (StorageCafe). Street rates softened — Fayetteville posted the second-largest YoY drop among major US cities at **-7.6% to $108** (RentCafe, June 2025) — but Raleigh-Durham and Charlotte showed month-over-month rate increases in mid/late 2025 (Multi-Housing News). National advertised street rate turned slightly positive (+0.3% YoY Dec 2025). Read: metro-dependent, generally **BALANCED with oversupply watch** in the Triad and Fayetteville.

#### Industrial / Warehouse (signature asset class)

**Charlotte industrial** (I-77/I-85 Southeast distribution hub) absorbed **1.9M sf in Q1 2026**, with **vacancy down to 7.3%** (down 140 bps YoY) even as the construction pipeline grew 112.6% off a low base (CBRE, Q1 2026). Total vacancy had risen to a 10-year high (10.5%) in early 2025 (Avison Young) before recovering. Charlotte led national absorption gains among top markets in Q1 2026 (Cushman & Wakefield). Read: **BALANCED**, recovering.

**Piedmont Triad industrial** is transformed by megaprojects: the **Toyota Battery Manufacturing plant in Liberty (Randolph County) — $13.9 billion, ~5,100 jobs, 1,850 acres, 30 GWh annual capacity** — began production in 2025 (NC Commerce/Toyota, Nov 12, 2025). The Triad also hosts the FedEx hub at Piedmont Triad International Airport and Boom Supersonic's manufacturing site at PTI. Read: **UNDERSUPPLIED→BALANCED** as build-to-suit and supplier space fills in.

**Chatham County / EV-semiconductor cluster** is the cautionary tale. **Wolfspeed** (silicon carbide semiconductors, Siler City/Chatham; Durham HQ) filed Chapter 11 on **June 30, 2025**, carrying ~$6.5B debt against ~$1.3B cash, with a prepackaged plan converting the company to Delaware incorporation (SEC filings; TechXplore, July 2025). **VinFast's** ~$4 billion Chatham EV plant was **delayed from 2025 to 2028** (Carolina Journal); NC filed suit in May 2026 over the missed operations deadline (Chatham Journal, May 2026), and VinFast shifted focus to Asian markets (Indonesia groundbreaking Aug 2025). Read: demand real but timing-fragile; **do not underwrite peak projected employment**.

**Raleigh-Durham industrial** is an emerging market servicing advanced manufacturing (CBRE lists Raleigh-Durham among emerging manufacturing-linked markets). Read: **BALANCED/UNDERSUPPLIED**.

#### Office (where distinctive)

**Charlotte office** carries genuine distress. Overall vacancy ended 2024 at 24.6% (Cushman & Wakefield) and stood at **24.2% in Q1 2026** after peaking mid-2025 (Cushman & Wakefield). Uptown vacancy was ~24.8% (CBRE Q3 2025). Flight-to-quality is real but incomplete — Avison Young data showed **2020s-vintage buildings at 23.7% vacancy**, higher than many older buildings (WFAE, July 2025). Older towers (the former Duke Energy HQ, the Johnston Building) are converting to apartments/hotels. Read: **OVERSUPPLIED / distress**.

**Raleigh-Durham office** carries ~20% traditional-office vacancy (CBRE) but benefits from lab-conversion optionality. Read: **BALANCED**, improving.

**Triangle life-science lab space** — the deep story — built enormous speculative capacity during the 2021–2023 biotech boom. Vacancy rose to **14.3% (Q2 2024)** from 8.7% a year earlier (CBRE); national lab vacancy hit ~23.5–27% (JLL, 2025). But the Triangle has outperformed: **1.1M+ sf of net absorption YTD in 2025, highest of any midsize market** (JLL 2025 Life Science report). Spec lab is **DIGESTING**; purpose-built biomanufacturing is a separate, still-tight story (below).

#### Hotels

**Asheville** is the distinctive post-disaster story. Hotel occupancy fell to **66.5% (early 2025) from 73.4%** a year earlier, with **RevPAR down 19% to $115** (WNC Business, July 2025); FY25 hotel room revenue and lodging tax were projected down 9% and 24% (Explore Asheville). Early-2025 occupancy was propped up by FEMA recovery workers and displaced residents on TSA vouchers — **not leisure spend** — masking a deeper leisure decline (Explore Asheville). Buncombe lodging-tax forecast was cut to **$22.8M (FY25) from $34.3M** expected. Recovery is projected but uneven; visitor spending was forecast +3.5% (2025) and +5.2% (2026). **Charlotte** (banking/convention/NASCAR/sports), **Raleigh-Durham** (tech/RTP/universities), and **Wilmington** (coastal tourism) are the healthier hotel markets. FLAG: metro-level STR/CoStar RevPAR and Lodging Econometrics pipeline counts for Charlotte/Raleigh not verified to a named source at publication.

#### Niche

**(a) Senior housing / skilled nursing.** NC's strict CON caps nursing-home and adult-care beds (supply-constrained, low oversupply risk). Assisted living (non-skilled) is less gated. FLAG: NIC MAP occupancy figures for Charlotte/Raleigh not verified to a named source at publication.

**(b) Student housing.** Major demand anchors: UNC-Chapel Hill, NC State (Raleigh), Duke (Durham), UNC-Charlotte, Wake Forest (Winston-Salem), East Carolina (Greenville), Appalachian State (Boone), UNC-Greensboro, and NC A&T (Greensboro). The Triangle's university pipeline is a structural multifamily demand driver (Northmarq). FLAG: purpose-built student housing occupancy/pre-leasing figures per campus not individually verified.

**(c) Life sciences / biomanufacturing — MAJOR THEME.** The Research Triangle is the #1 US biomanufacturing cluster (NCBiotech). NC booked **>$10 billion in life-science investment in 2024** (NCBiotech). Landmark projects: **Fujifilm Biotechnologies' $3.2 billion Holly Springs plant** (grand opening Sept 24, 2025; phase 1 = 8×20,000L bioreactors; opened with 680+ employees rising to 1,400 by 2031; Gov. Stein projected the overall project will "grow our state's economy by $4.7 billion over the next decade," with anchor tenants Regeneron ($3B/10-yr) and J&J ($2B/10-yr)); **Novo Nordisk's $4.1 billion Clayton expansion** (largest life-science capital investment in state history, NCBiotech); plus Amgen, Eli Lilly, Thermo Fisher, and others. NCBiotech's directory lists 840 life-science companies employing 75,000+. This owner-occupied/credit-tenant biomanufacturing demand is distinct from — and far healthier than — the speculative multi-tenant lab market (DIGESTING; see Office).

**(d) Data centers.** NC is a growing data-center market (Maiden hosts Apple's facility; the Charlotte–Triangle corridor is a recruitment zone). Duke Energy's **2025 Carolinas Resource Plan** (filed Oct 1, 2025) projects Carolinas load growth of ~35,000 GWh over 15 years — eight times the prior 15 years' rate — driven substantially by data centers; Duke disclosed a **73% YoY increase in data-center power requests in early 2026** (Tech Insider, 2026). Read: **UNDERSUPPLIED / high-growth**, gated by power/interconnection.

**(e) EV/battery/semiconductor manufacturing real estate.** Covered under Industrial — Toyota (ramping), Wolfspeed (bankruptcy), VinFast (delayed).

**(f) Car wash.** FLAG: No NC-specific car-wash supply or transaction data verified to a named source at publication.

**Signature NC angle — Live Oak Bank.** **Live Oak Bank, headquartered in Wilmington, NC, is the national #1 SBA 7(a) lender by dollar volume**, with **$2.8 billion across 2,280 loans in FY2025, up 44% YoY** (Live Oak press release, Oct 6, 2025; Coleman Report FY25) — roughly **$822 million ahead of #2 Newtek Bank ($2.0B)**, at an average loan size of ~$1.25M. Live Oak reclaimed the #1 spot after ranking #2 in FY2024, having held #1 for six consecutive years (FY2018–FY2023) (WilmingtonBiz, Oct 2025). Acquisition lending was ~33% of its FY2025 book. This makes Wilmington an outsized node in the national SBA system and a natural anchor for the NC funding-routing narrative. Other NC-HQ heavyweights: **First Citizens Bank** (Raleigh; acquired SVB in 2023), **Truist** (Charlotte; BB&T+SunTrust), and **Bank of America** (Charlotte).

FLAG: The REAP grant program was paused pending new regulations announced March 31, 2026 (the guaranteed-loan portion remains open); NC-specific B&I FY2025/FY2026 guarantee percentages not verified to a named USDA source. **Routing map (plain statement).**
- **Owner-occupied commercial real estate & equipment (small business), especially acquisitions** → SBA 7(a) (route first to Live Oak/Wilmington and other PLP lenders) or SBA 504 via a CDC (BEFCOR statewide, Carolina Business Capital). Combined 7(a)/504 cap rose to $10M effective July 4, 2026 (see Supporting Authority Data).
- **Rural east & west (population <50,000 towns), agriculture-linked, energy** → USDA B&I / REAP / Community Facilities via USDA RD Raleigh; heaviest fit in the rural east (hogs/poultry) and Helene-affected west.
- **Large multifamily, industrial, hotels, life-science** → conventional/CMBS; agency debt (Fannie/Freddie) for stabilized multifamily.
- **Geographic split:** Triangle deals skew life-science/tech/conventional; Charlotte skews banking/industrial/conventional+CMBS; the rural east and west skew USDA.

### LAYER B: North Carolina-Specific Rejection / Failure Forensics

1. **Statewide-average error.** Blending the booming Triangle/Charlotte, the moderate Triad, the flat/shrinking rural east, and the Helene-devastated west into one number is meaningless. Concrete: **Durham-Chapel Hill median rent +14.8% (2024→2025)** while **Charlotte rents fell 1.3% YoY and Raleigh ~0.7% YoY** in the same window (Realtor.com/Unicorn Rentals; Northmarq; Yardi Matrix). One state, opposite directions.

2. **Oversupply blindness (SIGNATURE).** Underwriting 2021-era Sun Belt/biotech rent growth into Charlotte or Raleigh multifamily — or into Triangle spec lab — is the classic failure. Charlotte vacancy rose to **8.2% (Q3 2025)** on 16,700+ (2024) and 16,200+ (2025) deliveries with rents **-1.3% YoY**; Raleigh-Durham vacancy hit **~8–11%** on its largest-ever supply wave; Triangle lab vacancy rose to **14.3% (Q2 2024)** from 8.7%. All are DIGESTING, not growing.

3. **Hurricane Helene / western-NC inland-flood catastrophe (SIGNATURE, UNDER-APPRECIATED).** Helene (Sept 27, 2024) caused catastrophic deadly flooding far inland across western NC — Asheville, Buncombe County, and the mountain counties. NC's Office of State Budget and Management put total damage and needs at **$59.6 billion** as of December 2024 — comprising **$44.4B direct damage, $9.4B indirect/induced, and $5.8B mitigation investment** — roughly 3.5x Hurricane Florence's $16.7B (2018), and the costliest disaster in state history. The defining underwriting fact: per NC Newsline (Apr 22, 2025), only **~5.2% of homes and ~10.2% of commercial properties** in the affected region carried FEMA flood insurance; Buncombe County had just ~1,295 NFIP policies (FEMA), and Reuters found roughly **1-in-200 homes** in the hardest-hit counties were NFIP-covered. UNC's Donald Hornstein estimated **~$9.5 billion in uninsured residential losses** (Carolina Public Press). This is the single most overlooked NC underwriting risk: inland flood in a market long perceived as "low-risk."

4. **Megaproject-timing / absorption error.** Toyota's $13.9B Liberty plant is ramping (production began 2025, 5,100 jobs targeted), but **Wolfspeed filed Chapter 11 (June 30, 2025)** and **VinFast's Chatham plant slipped to 2028**. Sizing workforce housing or supplier-warehouse absorption to peak projected employment — before it materializes, or when a project fails outright — is dangerous. Underwrite to contracted, in-place employment only.

5. **Rural/shrinking-market capture-rate error.** Many eastern NC agricultural counties and some western counties are flat or losing population; rural northeastern NC continues to lag (Carolina Journal/Census). Applying metro capture rates to these markets overstates absorbable demand. (Counterweight: coastal Brunswick/Pender and Triangle-exurb Johnston/Harnett are booming.)

6. **Insurance / climate mispricing (coastal AND inland).** NC carries coastal hurricane exposure (Outer Banks, Wilmington; the NC Insurance Underwriting Association "Beach Plan") and, post-Helene, proven inland flood exposure. The NC Rate Bureau sought a **42.2% average statewide homeowners increase (up to 99.4% on the coast)**; Commissioner Causey negotiated it down to **7.5% (June 1, 2025) + 7.5% (June 1, 2026)**, capped at 35% per territory (NC DOI, Jan 17, 2025). Coastal territories still absorb ~16% + ~15.9%. A separate dwelling-policy fight — the Rate Bureau sought **68.3%**, settled at **5% + 5%** (NC DOI, April 22, 2026) — confirms the trend. Property insurance is rising and must be stress-tested in NOI.

7. **Duke Energy / data-center / energy dynamics.** Duke Energy's 2025 Carolinas Resource Plan projects Carolinas load growth **8x the prior 15-year rate**, driven substantially by data centers and manufacturing; Duke plans a record ~$103 billion capital program and disclosed a **73% YoY jump in data-center power requests in early 2026** (Duke Energy; Fortune, April 2026; Tech Insider). SB 266 (2025) removed the 2030 interim carbon target while keeping 2050. Rate pressure (Duke sought a ~15% hike plus ~$800M fuel costs) and interconnection timelines are now feasibility variables for power-intensive projects.

### LAYER C: North Carolina Certificate-of-Need Precision

North Carolina is one of the **strictest and broadest FULL-CON states**, administered by the **NC DHHS Division of Health Service Regulation, Healthcare Planning and Certificate of Need Section**, under **NCGS Chapter 131E, Article 9**. CON is required for a very broad range: hospitals and hospital beds, nursing-home and adult-care-home beds, ambulatory surgical centers/facilities, major medical equipment (MRI, CT, PET, linear accelerators), home health, hospice, dialysis stations, and numerous services.

**Recent reform.** **House Bill 76 (Session Law 2023-7)**, signed March 27, 2023 as part of the Medicaid expansion deal, made substantial CON changes — eliminating CON for some services (e.g., chemical-dependency beds), raising capital-expenditure and equipment thresholds, and phasing in ASC/equipment exemptions in more populous counties on a tiered, multi-year schedule (2023–2025+) (Wyrick Robbins, 2023). It did **not** repeal CON.

**Constitutional litigation — Singleton v. NCDHHS (260PA22).** New Bern ophthalmologist Jay Singleton (represented by the Institute for Justice) challenges CON under the NC Constitution's Monopolies, Exclusive Emoluments, and Law-of-the-Land clauses. On **Oct 18, 2024**, the NC Supreme Court ruled the case was both a facial and as-applied challenge and remanded it to a **three-judge Wake County Superior Court panel**. On **December 19, 2025 (reported Dec 24)**, that panel ruled **unanimously against Singleton**, upholding the CON law; Singleton's counsel announced they will **appeal back toward the NC Supreme Court** (Carolina Public Press/NC Health News, Dec 2025). As of Q2 2026 the CON law **stands**, but the constitutional question remains live and could return to the Supreme Court.

**Further legislative activity.** Repeal/reform bills continued in the 2025–2026 session (e.g., a Senate budget provision proposing CON repeal — SB 370; the House version did not repeal). FLAG: final 2025–2027 budget CON outcome not confirmed to a named source at publication.

**Feasibility implication.** Healthcare supply is heavily gated — **low oversupply risk, CON a major barrier** — matching the New York/Illinois full-CON profile. NC retains strict CON for **nursing-home/adult-care beds** in particular. But HB 76's phased exemptions and the Singleton litigation create genuine loosening/uncertainty to track. **Competitors stating "NC has no CON" are catastrophically wrong; those citing only pre-2023 rules miss HB 76 and Singleton.**
